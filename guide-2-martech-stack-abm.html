Stack Layers

Understanding the architecture of modern martech

TL;DR
  • Collection Layer: Where data enters (tracking, forms, enrichment, intent signals)
  • Storage Layer: Where data lives (CRM, CDP, data warehouse)
  • Activation Layer: Where you act on data (MAP, email, ads, sales engagement)
  • Measurement Layer: Where you analyze results (attribution, dashboards, BI tools)
üìç Scenario: The Architecture Question
MC

Maya Chen

Marketing Ops Manager, NovaTech

"Does it collect data, store data, activate campaigns, or measure results?"

Maya Chen is helping evaluate a new tool that promises to "accelerate pipeline." The vendor demo is impressive, but Maya's first question isn't about features‚Äîit's about architecture. "Which layer does this live in?" she asks. The vendor says it "integrates with everything." Maya pushes: "Does it collect data, store data, activate campaigns, or measure results?" Turns out it's trying to do all four‚Äîa red flag for complexity and overlap with existing tools.

üí° THE TAKEAWAY

Understanding layers helps you evaluate tools and diagnose problems faster.

The Four-Layer Model

Layer Functions at a Glance
Layer Purpose Example Tools
Collection Data enters the stack Segment, forms, intent providers
Storage Data lives here Salesforce, Snowflake, CDP
Activation Act on the data Marketo, HubSpot, Outreach
Measurement Analyze results Bizible, Tableau, Looker
Deep Dive: Common Layer Mismatches

Most martech problems stem from using tools at the wrong layer:

  • Measuring with activation tools: Trying to get attribution data from your MAP. It wasn't designed for that‚Äîuse a dedicated measurement tool.
  • Storing in collection tools: Treating your web analytics as a customer database. It captures events, not relationships.
  • Activating from storage: Running campaigns directly from your CRM or data warehouse. These are systems of record, not engagement engines.
  • Collecting at the storage layer: Expecting your CRM to automatically pull third-party data. You need enrichment tools at the collection layer.

The fix: When evaluating any tool, first answer: "Which layer does this belong to?" If it claims to do everything, be skeptical about its depth in any single layer.

Knowledge Check

A vendor says their tool "captures website behavior, stores unified profiles, sends personalized emails, and provides attribution reports." What's the likely problem with this tool?

Correct! When a tool claims to operate across all four layers (collection, storage, activation, and measurement), it's a red flag. Best-in-class tools typically excel in one or two layers. Tools that try to do everything often do nothing well.

Not quite. The key issue is that the tool spans all four stack layers. When a tool claims to collect, store, activate, AND measure, it likely lacks depth in any single area. Specialized tools typically outperform all-in-one solutions.

Deep Dive: Making Analytics Work for B2B

Standard analytics tools are built for B2C (individual users). To make them useful for B2B:

  1. Identify users early: Gate valuable content to capture email, then associate sessions with known contacts
  2. Use IP-to-company enrichment: Tools like Clearbit Reveal or 6sense can identify company from IP address (works for ~30-40% of traffic)
  3. Pass data to warehouse: Export analytics data to your warehouse, join with CRM data there
  4. Track key business events: Beyond page views‚Äîdemo requests, pricing page visits, docs engagement, trial signups

The goal: Connect anonymous web behavior ‚Üí known contacts ‚Üí CRM opportunities ‚Üí revenue.

Evaluating Vendors

No enrichment vendor has perfect data. The decision usually comes down to:

  • Match rate: What percentage of your records can they enrich?
  • Accuracy: Of enriched records, what percentage is correct?
  • Freshness: How recently was the data verified?
  • Coverage: Do they have data for your target segments? (SMB vs enterprise, US vs international, specific industries)
Deep Dive: Vendor Evaluation Process

Practical Approach: Before signing a contract, give vendors a sample of 500-1000 records. Manually verify a subset of their enrichment. This reveals real match rates and accuracy better than any sales deck.

Evaluation Checklist:

  1. Sample test: Send 500+ records from your actual database
  2. Manual verification: Spot-check 50-100 enriched records on LinkedIn
  3. Segment analysis: Check match rates by geography, company size, industry
  4. API performance: Test real-time latency if doing form-fill enrichment
  5. Integration check: Confirm they connect to your specific CRM/MAP versions
  6. Contract terms: Understand credit usage, overage costs, data refresh policies

Identity Resolution and Account Matching

The hardest problem in B2B martech‚Äîconnecting the dots across systems

‚ö° TL;DR

The same person appears in your systems under different identifiers (cookies, emails, names). Identity resolution connects these fragments into unified profiles. Approaches: deterministic (exact match), probabilistic (fuzzy match), or graph-based (CDPs). Contact-to-account matching is even harder‚Äî20-30% of B2B leads use personal email. Prevention beats cure: dedupe at entry, not after the fact.

The Identity Problem

One person might appear in your stack as:

Website: Cookie ID abc123 (anonymous)
Website: Cookie ID xyz789 (different device)
Form fill: [email protected]

üìã Quick Reference: Identity Resolution Approaches

Deterministic Matching: Exact identifiers (same email = same person) ‚Üí Simple but limited ‚Üí Misses cases where identifiers differ

Probabilistic Matching: Signals that suggest identity (same name + company + similar email) ‚Üí More powerful ‚Üí Can create false positives

Graph-Based: Build connection graph (cookie A ‚Üí email X ‚Üí account Y) ‚Üí Most comprehensive ‚Üí CDPs typically use this approach

Contact-to-Account Matching

Even harder than person matching is matching contacts to accounts. Challenges:

  • Email domains don't match: Person uses gmail.com, not company domain
  • Subsidiaries: "Acme Widgets" is part of "Acme Corp" but has different domain
  • Rebrands: Company changed name but old references persist
  • Common names: Multiple companies named "Apex Technologies"

Practical Matching Logic

function matchContactToAccount(contact):
    # Step 1: Email domain match
    domain = extractDomain(contact.email)
    if domain not in GENERIC_DOMAINS:  # gmail, yahoo, etc.
        matches = findAccountsByDomain(domain)
        if matches.length == 1:
            return matches[0]
    
    # Step 2: Company name fuzzy match
    if contact.companyName:
        matches = fuzzyMatchAccountName(contact.companyName)
        if matches.length == 1 and matches[0].score > 0.9:
            return matches[0]
    
    # Step 3: Enrichment lookup
    enrichedData = enrichmentAPI.lookup(contact.email)
    if enrichedData.companyId:
        account = findAccountByEnrichmentId(enrichedData.companyId)
        if account:
            return account
    
    # Step 4: Manual review queue
    return addToReviewQueue(contact)
Generic Domains: About 20-30% of B2B leads use personal email (gmail, yahoo, outlook). You cannot match these by domain. Either require work email on forms (loses conversions) or accept that some leads won't auto-match to accounts.

Deduplication

The flip side of identity resolution: finding and merging duplicate records.

Matching criteria for duplicates:

  • Exact email match (easiest)
  • Same name + same company (probable duplicate)
  • Same name + similar email domain (possible duplicate)
  • Same phone number (strong signal)

Merge rules: When merging duplicates, you need rules for which value wins. Common approach: prefer most recently updated, but keep older created date. Always preserve the record with most activity/history.

Prevention > Cure: Dedupe at point of entry. Check for existing records before creating new ones in forms and imports. Deduping after the fact is painful‚Äîleads lose history, campaign membership gets confused, sales complains about missing data.
Deep Dive: Deduplication Best Practices

Matching Criteria for Duplicates

  • Exact email match: Easiest, most reliable
  • Same name + same company: Probable duplicate, verify before merging
  • Same name + similar email domain: Possible duplicate, manual review recommended
  • Same phone number: Strong signal, but verify (phone numbers get recycled)

Merge Rules

When merging duplicates, you need clear rules for which value wins:

  • Prefer most recently updated field values
  • Keep the older created date (preserves history)

Data Hygiene and Governance

Fighting the constant decay of your database

‚ö° TL;DR

Data quality degrades constantly‚Äî22-25% of emails become invalid annually, 20-25% of job titles change. Focus on four dimensions: completeness, accuracy, consistency, and timeliness. Normalize critical fields (country codes, job levels, industries). Treat data hygiene as ongoing process, not one-time project. Build hygiene into entry points: validate on capture, enrich regularly, dedupe continuously.

üìã Quick Reference: Data Quality Dimensions

Completeness: Are required fields filled? Target >90% for critical fields (email, company), >70% for enrichment fields (industry, size)

Accuracy: Are values correct? Watch for "asdfasdf" job titles, "test company" accounts‚Äîrequires sampling and manual verification

Consistency: Same thing, same way. "United States" vs "US" vs "USA" breaking reports. Picklists help.

Timeliness: How fresh? A 2019 lead has probably changed jobs. Track last_verified dates and re-verify stale records.

Normalization Essentials

Country/State: Use ISO codes (US, not "United States"). Pick one format and convert everything.

Job titles: Standardize to levels (Individual Contributor, Manager, Director, VP, C-level) and functions (Marketing, Sales, IT, Finance). Raw titles are too varied for segmentation.

Industry: Map to a standard taxonomy (NAICS, SIC, or your own). "Tech" and "Technology" and "Software" should resolve to the same value.

Company names: Harder. "IBM" vs "International Business Machines" vs "IBM Corp." Consider using domain as canonical identifier instead.

Before normalization:
  title: "sr. marketing manager, digital"
  country: "United States of America"
  industry: "Tech/SaaS"
  
After normalization:
  title_raw: "sr. marketing manager, digital"
  title_level: "Manager"
  title_function: "Marketing"
  country_code: "US"
  industry: "Software"

  • Field ownership: Who can edit which fields? Marketing shouldn't modify sales fields and vice versa.
  • Validation rules: Enforce formats at point of entry (email format, phone format, required fields).
  • Regular audits: Monthly or quarterly data quality reviews. Check fill rates, uniqueness, validity.
  • Archival policy: What happens to old, unengaged leads? Define aging rules and suppress/archive accordingly.

Privacy and Consent

Navigating regulations and the end of third-party cookies

‚ö° TL;DR

GDPR requires opt-in for EU contacts; CCPA allows opt-out. Third-party cookies are dying‚ÄîSafari/Firefox already block them, Chrome's Privacy Sandbox is evolving. Retargeting, cross-site attribution, and multi-touch tracking are breaking. Shift to first-party data: capture identity earlier, build direct relationships, accept measurement uncertainty. Perfect tracking is ending.

PS

üìã Quick Reference: Key Privacy Regulations

GDPR (EU): Explicit opt-in required ‚Ä¢ Must honor deletion requests ‚Ä¢ Can't transfer EU data without protection ‚Ä¢ Pre-checked boxes don't count as consent

CCPA/CPRA (California): Opt-out model (can market until they say stop) ‚Ä¢ Must provide "do not sell" option ‚Ä¢ Must disclose what you collect

Others: LGPD (Brazil), PIPEDA (Canada), proliferating US state laws ‚Ä¢ Trend is toward more restriction

Cookie Deprecation

Third-party cookies (used for cross-site tracking and ad targeting) are being blocked by browsers:

Browser Status Impact
Safari Blocked since 2020 ~20% of traffic affected
Firefox Blocked ~5% of traffic
Chrome Evolving (Privacy Sandbox) ~65% of traffic‚Äîthis is the big one

What breaks without third-party cookies:

  • Retargeting ads (can't follow users across sites)
  • Cross-site attribution (can't connect ad impression to website visit)
  • Third-party data enrichment (can't match anonymous visitors)
  • Multi-touch attribution (harder to track the full journey)

Practical Implications for B2B Marketing

Consent Management

You need a system to capture, store, and respect consent preferences. Cookie consent banners are the visible part, but you also need:

  • Consent stored in your database (not just cookie)
  • Ability to suppress marketing to non-consented contacts
  • Audit trail of consent (when, how, what they agreed to)
  • Integration with your MAP to respect preferences

First-Party Data Strategy

As third-party data becomes less available, first-party data becomes more valuable:

  • Invest in capturing email/identity earlier in the journey
  • Build direct relationships (newsletters, communities, events)
  • Capture zero-party data (preferences explicitly shared)
  • Product usage data (for SaaS) becomes key signal

Attribution Challenges

Multi-touch attribution depends on tracking. As tracking becomes harder:

  • Self-reported attribution ("how did you hear about us?") becomes more important
  • Marketing mix modeling (statistical approach) gains relevance
  • Lift testing and incrementality become gold standard
  • Accept that you won't have perfect visibility‚Äîbuild for directional insight
Realistic View: Perfect tracking is ending. The companies that win will be those that build direct relationships, capture first-party data thoughtfully, and accept measurement uncertainty rather than pretending they have perfect attribution.

Account-Based Marketing

Flip the funnel‚Äîstart with target accounts, not lead volume

‚ö° TL;DR

ABM is a strategy, not a tool. Start with target accounts (tiered: 1:1 for top 10-50, 1:Few for 50-500, 1:Many for 500+), coordinate channels around them, and measure account engagement rather than lead volume. ABM requires sales buy-in‚Äîit fails when marketing picks accounts unilaterally. Common mistake: too many "Tier 1" accounts. If you have 500 Tier 1 accounts, you have zero.

ABM Orchestration

ABM requires coordinating multiple channels for the same accounts:

ABM Tech Stack Components

  • Account data/targeting: 6sense, Demandbase, Terminus (identify, score, target accounts)
  • Account-based advertising: LinkedIn, display through ABM platforms
  • Personalization: Mutiny, Intellimize (customize web experience per account)
  • Direct mail: Sendoso, Postal.io (physical touches at scale)
  • Sales engagement: Outreach, Salesloft (coordinate sales plays)
  • Intent signals: Bombora, G2 (prioritize accounts showing buying signals)

ABM Measurement

ABM changes how you measure. Lead volume is less relevant when you're targeting 200 specific accounts.

Key ABM metrics:

  • Account coverage: % of target accounts with known contacts
  • Account engagement: Multi-person engagement within account (not just one contact)
  • Pipeline per account: Average pipeline generated from target accounts
  • Velocity: How fast do engaged accounts move through pipeline?
  • Target account pipeline %: What portion of total pipeline is from target accounts?
  • Win rate: Do target accounts close at higher rates?
In Practice: ABM works when sales and marketing agree on target accounts and collaborate continuously. It fails when marketing picks accounts unilaterally and throws leads over the wall. If your sales team isn't bought in, ABM is just expensive advertising.
Deep Dive: Common ABM Mistakes
  • Too many Tier 1 accounts: If you have 500 "Tier 1" accounts, you have zero Tier 1 accounts. 1:1 doesn't scale.
  • No sales buy-in: Marketing can't run ABM alone. Sales must commit to working the accounts.
  • Lead metrics for ABM: Reporting MQL volume for an ABM program misses the point.
  • No account-level tracking: If your systems can't aggregate contacts to accounts, you can't measure ABM.
  • Spray and pray with ads: Running ads to target accounts isn't ABM‚Äîit's just targeting. ABM requires orchestration.

How to assess what you have and plan what you need

‚ö° TL;DR
