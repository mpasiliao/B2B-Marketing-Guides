B2B vs B2C Differences

Why everything you learned about consumer marketing needs to be rethought for B2B.

‚ö° TL;DR ‚Äî 30-Second Summary
  • B2B involves 3-10+ decision makers vs. B2C's single buyer
  • Sales cycles run 30-365+ days, not minutes
  • Deal values are $10K-$1M+, requiring ROI justification
  • Attribution is fundamentally different‚Äîthe ad clicker rarely signs the contract
  • Never apply B2C measurement timelines to B2B campaigns
üìç Scenario: The Facebook Ads Reality Check
MC

Maya Chen

Marketing Ops Manager, NovaTech

"Priya, I know you crushed it with Facebook ads at your last internship. But I need you to forget almost everything you learned there. Here's why..."

Priya Sharma, NovaTech's new data analyst, had just joined from a summer internship at a D2C skincare brand. She'd run Facebook campaigns that generated 4x ROAS in two weeks. Her first suggestion at NovaTech? "Why don't we scale up our Facebook spend? I can build the same attribution models I used before."

Maya pulled up her laptop. "Let me show you our last closed deal. First touch: a LinkedIn ad in March. The person who clicked? A junior engineer researching tools for a hackathon project. He mentioned it to his manager in April. The manager downloaded our ROI calculator in May. Their VP attended our webinar in June. IT security ran their evaluation in July. Legal negotiated for three weeks in August. We closed in September‚Äîsix months after that first ad click."

Maya continued: "If we measured that LinkedIn ad's performance two weeks after it ran? Zero revenue. Zero pipeline. The 'failure' we'd have killed was actually the first domino in a $42,000 deal."

üí° THE TAKEAWAY

B2B attribution doesn't work on B2C timelines. The person who clicks your ad is rarely the person who signs the contract, and measuring too early makes winning campaigns look like losers.

What This Means for Marketing

Content depth matters. B2B buyers need ammunition to make their case internally. A 30-second video might sell sneakers, but enterprise software requires whitepapers, case studies, ROI calculators, and technical documentation. Your champion needs to convince their CFO, their IT team, and their legal department‚Äîthey need more than a catchy tagline.

Multi-threading is essential. You're not marketing to a person‚Äîyou're marketing to a buying committee. Each stakeholder has different concerns. IT cares about security and integrations. Finance cares about TCO and payment terms. End users care about ease of use. One message won't work for all.

Relationships compound. B2B customers sign multi-year contracts and may stay 5+ years. The lifetime value of keeping a customer happy vastly exceeds acquisition costs. Marketing doesn't stop at the sale‚Äîcustomer marketing, advocacy programs, and expansion campaigns drive serious revenue.

Deep Dive: Attribution Timelines and Why They Break in B2B

The fundamental problem: B2C attribution models assume the person who sees an ad is the person who buys. In B2B, this is almost never true.

Consider a typical B2B journey:

  • First touch: Junior analyst clicks a LinkedIn ad (March)
  • Multiple touches: Manager downloads whitepaper, VP attends webinar (April-June)
  • Opportunity creation: Sales starts working the deal (July)
  • Closed-won: Contract signed (September)

If you're using 30-day attribution windows or measuring campaign ROI monthly, that LinkedIn ad shows zero return. But it was the catalyst for a $42K deal.

Recommended approach:

  • Use 90-180 day attribution windows minimum
  • Track at the account level, not just the lead level
  • Measure leading indicators (MQLs, pipeline) before lagging indicators (revenue)
  • Accept that some impact will never be directly attributable‚Äîdemand gen works even when you can't prove it
‚úÖ Knowledge Check

You're running a LinkedIn campaign for a $50K ACV product. Your CFO asks why the campaign shows no revenue after 6 weeks. What's the best response?

At $50K ACV, sales cycles typically run 3-6 months. Measuring revenue after 6 weeks will almost always show zero. The right approach is to show leading indicators like MQLs, SQLs, and pipeline created‚Äîand educate stakeholders on appropriate measurement timelines for B2B.

ICP & Buyer Personas

Define who you're selling to‚Äîat the company level and the human level.

‚ö° TL;DR ‚Äî 30-Second Summary
  • ICP (Ideal Customer Profile) defines which companies to target‚Äîfirmographics + technographics
  • Buyer Personas define which people within those companies influence the purchase
  • Build ICP from your best customers (highest NPS, lowest churn, largest deals)
  • Anti-ICP is just as important‚Äîknow who to avoid
  • Personas must be validated with data, not imagined in a conference room
üìç Scenario: The "Target Everyone" Trap
DW

Derek Williams

VP of Marketing, NovaTech

"Why are we limiting ourselves? Let's just target everyone who might need project management software. Cast a wider net!"

Derek had just come from a board meeting where the message was clear: grow faster. His instinct was to expand targeting. "We're leaving money on the table," he told Maya. "Let's go after companies of all sizes."

Maya pulled up a Looker dashboard she'd been dreading to share. "Derek, I ran the numbers on our last 200 closed deals. Look at the pattern." The data was brutal: companies under 150 employees churned at 3x the rate of mid-market. Companies over 3,000 employees had 8-month sales cycles and required enterprise features NovaTech didn't have. The sweet spot‚Äî200 to 2,000 employees‚Äîhad 40% higher LTV and 60% faster sales cycles.

"We tried 'target everyone' last Q2," Maya continued. "We spent $80K on campaigns reaching small businesses. We got 400 leads. 12 became customers. 9 churned within 6 months. Net revenue? Negative $15K after CAC."

Derek stared at the screen. "So when we target everyone, we actually lose money on most of them."

üí° THE TAKEAWAY

A tight ICP isn't a limitation‚Äîit's a multiplier. Targeting companies likely to succeed means higher win rates, lower churn, and better unit economics. "More leads" from bad-fit accounts is negative ROI.

NovaTech sells project management software for engineering teams at $35K ACV. Here's their validated ICP:

  • Enterprise 5,000+ (need features we don't have yet)

Deep Dive: Building Data-Driven Personas

The cardinal sin: Creating personas in a conference room based on assumptions. "Let's call her Marketing Mary‚Äîshe's 35, drinks lattes, and loves data." This is fiction, not strategy.

How to build validated personas:

  1. Interview 10-15 recent customers. Ask: How did you find us? What problem were you solving? Who else was involved in the decision? What almost stopped you from buying?
  2. Analyze closed-won deals. What titles were involved? What content did they consume? How long was the sales cycle?
  3. Talk to sales. Which objections come up repeatedly? What questions do different roles ask?
  4. Look at churned customers. Were they a certain persona type? Did they have different expectations?

Persona validation checklist:

  • Can you name 5+ real customers who fit this persona?
  • Does sales recognize this person from their calls?
  • Do the pain points come from actual quotes, not guesses?
  • Have you validated the content preferences with engagement data?
‚úÖ Knowledge Check

Your company's best customers are mid-market SaaS companies (200-1,000 employees). A new campaign generates 300 leads, but 80% are from companies under 50 employees. What should you do?

Lead volume from bad-fit accounts is vanity metrics. If your data shows small companies churn at high rates, those 240 leads represent wasted sales time and negative ROI. Fix the targeting, not the ICP. The ICP is based on where you actually win and retain‚Äîdon't let campaign results override customer success data.

Buying Committee / Decision-Making Unit

B2B deals involve 3-10+ people. Know who they are and what they need.

‚ö° TL;DR ‚Äî 30-Second Summary
  • B2B purchases involve a buying committee‚Äîmultiple stakeholders with different concerns
  • Key roles: Champion (advocate), Economic Buyer (budget), Technical Evaluator (specs), Blocker (risk)
  • Each role needs different content‚Äîone message doesn't fit all
  • Track engagement at the account level, not just individual leads
  • Enable your champion with internal-selling tools (battle cards, ROI calculators, slides)
üìç Scenario: The CDP Purchase Journey
SP

Sarah Park

Demand Gen Manager, NovaTech

"I spend 15 hours every week reconciling customer data across 8 different systems. I can't even answer basic questions about our customer journey."

Month 1 ‚Äî Problem Recognition: Sarah realizes their customer data is fragmented and starts researching CDPs. She downloads a whitepaper from Vendor A, reads G2 reviews, and asks for recommendations in a Marketing Ops Slack community.

Month 2 ‚Äî Building the Case: Sarah creates a business case and brings it to Derek, the VP of Marketing (Economic Buyer). Derek is interested but needs IT sign-off before committing budget.

Month 3 ‚Äî Technical Evaluation: Sarah loops in David, NovaTech's Data Engineer (Technical Evaluator). David's concerns: "How does this integrate with Snowflake? What's the data latency? Who manages the schema?" He needs technical documentation, not marketing materials.

Month 4 ‚Äî The Blocker: The deal is progressing until Jennifer from Security steps in. She requires SOC 2 Type II compliance, US data residency, and a completed security questionnaire. If Vendor A can't provide these, the deal is dead‚Äîno matter how much Sarah loves the product.

Month 5 ‚Äî Final Approval: Derek takes the recommendation to Tom Reeves, the CFO, for budget approval. Tom wants ROI projections. Legal reviews the MSA. After 4 months and 7 people across 4 departments, they sign.

üí° THE TAKEAWAY

If you only tracked Sarah's whitepaper download, you'd miss David's technical doc views and Jennifer's security page visits. Account-based measurement exists because of buying committees‚Äîmultiple touches across multiple people lead to one deal.

What This Means for Marketing

Multi-threaded campaigns are essential. You need content for each role in the committee. Technical docs for the evaluator. ROI calculators for the economic buyer. Security certifications for the blocker. One-size-fits-all content leaves gaps.

Track at the account level. When multiple people from the same company engage with your content, that's a buying signal. If you only track individual leads, you miss the pattern of a committee forming around a purchase.

Enable your champion. Your champion has to sell internally when you're not in the room. Give them tools: pre-built slides they can present, battle cards comparing you to alternatives, ROI frameworks they can customize for their CFO.

Deep Dive: Mapping Content to Committee Roles

When auditing your content library, map each asset to the committee roles it serves:

Content Type Primary Role Secondary Roles

ROI calculator Economic Buyer Champion

Content gap analysis: If you have 20 pieces of top-of-funnel content but zero security documentation, you'll generate interest but lose deals at the blocker stage.

‚úÖ Knowledge Check

A lead downloads 3 ebooks but they're a junior analyst at a target account. Their VP is the likely decision-maker. What's the risk?

Junior employees can be valuable champions, but they often lack the authority to push deals through. The risk is that you invest in nurturing someone who can't actually influence the purchase. Smart B2B marketing multi-threads: nurture the analyst AND run campaigns to engage the VP and other committee members at the same account.

The B2B Funnel

Track prospects from first touch to closed deal‚Äîand learn the math that makes it work.

‚ö° TL;DR ‚Äî 30-Second Summary
  • Standard stages: Visitor ‚Üí Lead ‚Üí MQL ‚Üí SQL ‚Üí Opportunity ‚Üí Closed Won
  • MQL = fits ICP + shows engagement. SQL = sales verified via conversation
  • Typical conversions: 2% visitor‚Üílead, 20% lead‚ÜíMQL, 50% MQL‚ÜíSQL, 25% opp‚Üíwon
  • Work backwards from revenue targets to calculate required top-of-funnel volume
  • If MQL‚ÜíSQL rate is below 40%, your MQL definition is too loose
üìç Scenario: The Great MQL Debate
JO

James Okonkwo

Sales Director, NovaTech

"Another 47 MQLs last week. I had my team call all of them. Want to guess how many were worth talking to? Eight. Eight actual conversations with someone who had budget and a real timeline."

Maya opened her dashboard. "Based on disposition data, 22 converted to SAL‚Äî"

"Eight," James interrupted. "The rest were students, competitors, or people who thought they were downloading a free template, not requesting a sales call."

Derek stepped in. "Our MQL numbers look great to the board‚Äî"

"The board doesn't have to make 40 calls to find 8 real prospects," James said. "My team is burning out on garbage leads."

Maya pulled up the lead scoring breakdown. The pattern was clear: 31 of the rejected leads had hit MQL threshold purely on content downloads‚Äîno pricing page visits, no demo requests, no buying signals. They'd accumulated points but showed zero intent.

"We're scoring engagement too heavily," Maya said. "Someone downloading 6 ebooks isn't necessarily ready to buy. Let's create a High-Intent MQL tier that requires at least one buying signal."

Three weeks later, sales follow-up time dropped from 18 hours to 4 hours. SAL conversion jumped from 17% to 58%.

üí° THE TAKEAWAY

MQL definitions aren't academic‚Äîthey directly impact sales efficiency and team morale. A loose MQL floods sales with noise. The best criteria balance volume with genuine buying signals.

What "Qualified" Actually Means

Marketing Qualified (MQL) ‚Äî meets basic criteria suggesting they could be a customer:

  • Fit: Company matches ICP (size, industry, geography)
  • Engagement: High-intent action (demo request, pricing page) OR sufficient lead score
  • Recency: Engaged recently, not 18 months ago

Sales Qualified (SQL) ‚Äî sales has confirmed genuine potential (often using BANT):

  • Budget: They have or can get funding
  • Authority: You're talking to someone who can influence the decision
  • Need: They have a real problem you solve
  • Timeline: Looking to decide in a reasonable timeframe

Deep Dive: Diagnosing Funnel Problems

When a funnel stage underperforms, diagnose before acting:

Problem Possible Causes Investigation
Low visitor‚Üílead Weak CTAs, bad traffic quality, friction in forms Check traffic sources, A/B test forms
Low lead‚ÜíMQL Wrong audience, MQL criteria too strict Analyze lead sources, review scoring model
Low MQL‚ÜíSQL MQL too loose, sales not following up, bad fit Review rejection reasons, check follow-up times
Low SQL‚ÜíOpp Poor discovery, wrong personas, pricing issues Listen to sales calls, review lost reasons
Low win rate Competitive losses, product gaps, pricing Win/loss analysis, competitive intel

Key rule: Don't optimize top-of-funnel if bottom-of-funnel is broken. Driving more visitors to a leaky funnel wastes budget.

‚úÖ Knowledge Check

Your MQL‚ÜíSQL rate drops from 50% to 30% over 2 months. What's the MOST likely explanation?

A conversion drop could stem from any of these causes. The first step is to segment: Is the drop across all sources or specific to one channel? Is sales follow-up time constant? Good analysts don't assume‚Äîthey diagnose. Check rejection reasons, source performance, and timing patterns before jumping to solutions.

Lead Lifecycle & Handoffs

Where deals die: the marketing-to-sales handoff and how to fix it.

‚ö° TL;DR ‚Äî 30-Second Summary
  • Lead states go beyond the funnel: New ‚Üí Working ‚Üí MQL ‚Üí SAL ‚Üí SQL ‚Üí (Won/Recycled/Disqualified)
  • Recycled ‚â† Disqualified: Recycled goes back to nurture; disqualified is permanent
  • Lead scoring combines fit (firmographics) + engagement (behavior) + negative signals
  • Four failure modes: neglect, no feedback, vanity scoring, no recycling
  • Require reason codes for all rejections‚Äîthey're gold for improving lead quality
üìç Scenario: The Leaky Handoff
MC

Maya Chen

Marketing Ops Manager, NovaTech

"We sent 156 MQLs to sales last month. According to the CRM, only 89 were ever contacted. The other 67 just... disappeared."

Maya had been digging into the pipeline gap. Marketing was hitting MQL targets, but somehow the numbers weren't translating to opportunities.

"Look at this one," she said, pulling up a lead record. "Perfect ICP fit. They downloaded our pricing guide, attended a webinar, AND submitted a demo request. They sat uncontacted for 11 days. By the time sales called, they'd already signed with a competitor."

James from sales pushed back: "We're drowning in leads. Half of them are garbage. We have to prioritize."

Maya pulled up another report. "But you're not just deprioritizing‚Äîyou're ignoring without leaving any feedback. 34 leads were marked 'Bad' with no reason code. I can't improve scoring if I don't know what's wrong."

They spent the next hour building a new system: tiered MQLs (Hot/Warm) with different SLAs, mandatory reason codes, and a weekly review of rejected leads. It wasn't perfect, but at least leads would stop falling into the void.

üí° THE TAKEAWAY

The handoff between marketing and sales is where leads go to die. Without visibility, accountability, and feedback loops, even great leads vanish. Track every transition and require explanations for every rejection.

Four Failure Modes (and How to Fix Them)

üö® Lead Neglect: MQLs sit untouched for days. By the time sales calls, the prospect went with a competitor.
Fix: Strict SLAs with escalation and manager visibility into compliance.
üö® Rejection Without Feedback: Sales marks leads "bad" without explaining why. Marketing can't improve.
Fix: Require reason codes; review rejection patterns monthly.
üö® Vanity Scoring: Student downloads 10 ebooks, gets high score. Never going to buy.
Fix: Weight high-intent actions (demo, pricing) over content consumption.
üö® No Recycling: "Not ready" leads get disqualified instead of recycled. Lost forever.
Fix: Make recycling the default, disqualification the exception.

Deep Dive: Building a Recycling Program

Why recycling matters: A lead that's "not ready now" might be ready in 6 months. If you disqualify them, you lose that opportunity. If you recycle them back to nurture, you stay top-of-mind until timing is right.

Recycling best practices:

  • Cooling-off period: Prevent immediate re-MQL. Wait 30-90 days before they can hit threshold again.
  • Score decay: Reduce engagement scores over time. A webinar from 6 months ago isn't as relevant today.
  • Re-engagement triggers: If a recycled lead visits the pricing page, fast-track them back to sales.
  • Reason-based nurture: "Not ready - timeline" gets different nurture than "Not ready - evaluating competitors".
‚úÖ Knowledge Check

A lead from a target account has a score of 45 (just under your 50-point MQL threshold). They work at a competitor. What should happen?

Negative signals should outweigh positive engagement. A competitor with 45 points minus the -100 competitor penalty = -55. They should be automatically disqualified (not recycled‚Äîthey'll never be a customer). This is why negative scoring is critical: it prevents sending obvious non-buyers to sales no matter how much content they consume.

Sales/Marketing SLAs

Formalize the handoff with mutual commitments and end the finger-pointing.

‚ö° TL;DR ‚Äî 30-Second Summary
  • An SLA creates mutual accountability: Marketing commits to lead quality, Sales commits to follow-up
  • Key marketing commitments: MQL volume, quality criteria, enrichment, routing accuracy
  • Key sales commitments: Follow-up time, contact attempts, disposition reasons, status updates
  • Build a shared dashboard both teams review weekly‚Äîvisibility creates accountability
  • Review and adjust quarterly based on actual performance
üìç Scenario: The Accountability Gap
TR

Tom Reeves

CFO, NovaTech

"We spent $400K on marketing last quarter. Sales says the leads were garbage. Marketing says sales didn't follow up. I don't know who to believe because nobody has data."

Tom had called an emergency meeting after Q3 pipeline came in 30% under target. The room was tense.

James from Sales opened: "Marketing gave us 800 MQLs. Half were students or competitors. The other half didn't have budget."

Derek from Marketing countered: "We hit our MQL targets. It's not our fault if sales takes two weeks to call them back."

Maya had been quietly pulling data. "Both of you are partially right," she said. "I can show you exactly where the breakdown happened." She shared her screen: 47% of MQLs were never contacted within 7 days. Of those contacted quickly, 61% converted to SQLs. Of those contacted after 7 days, only 18% converted.

"But," Maya continued, "23% of MQLs came from a content syndication vendor with a 6% SQL rate. We need to fix that source."

Tom nodded. "So we need commitments from both sides. Written down. With tracking."

üí° THE TAKEAWAY

Without an SLA and the data to track it, every pipeline miss turns into a blame game. Formalize commitments from both teams and make the metrics visible to leadership. Arguments disappear when replaced by data.

Deep Dive: Building SLA Dashboards

An effective SLA dashboard should be reviewed weekly by both teams and include:

Marketing accountability metrics:

  • MQL quality score (avg fit + engagement)
  • Source breakdown with conversion rates
  • Routing accuracy (% to correct rep)

Sales accountability metrics:

  • Avg time to first contact
  • % contacted within SLA
  • Avg contact attempts before disposition
  • % with rejection reason codes

  • MQL‚ÜíSQL rate by source
  • Pipeline generated from MQLs
  • Rejection reason distribution
‚úÖ Knowledge Check

Sales is rejecting 40% of MQLs with reason code "No Budget." Marketing says the leads are fine. Who's right, and what should happen?

"No budget" often means "not a priority right now" rather than "will never have budget." The right response is to dig deeper: Are these good-fit companies that need more nurturing? Are they truly outside your ICP's budget range? Are sales reps using "no budget" as a catch-all rejection? Without understanding the root cause, you can't fix the problem.

Demand Gen vs Lead Gen

Two different games with different rules‚Äîknow which one you're playing.

‚ö° TL;DR ‚Äî 30-Second Summary
  • Lead Gen = capture contact info (gated content, forms, events) ‚Äî measurable by CPL
  • Demand Gen = build awareness and preference (ungated content, podcasts, brand) ‚Äî shows up in dark funnel
  • Lead gen feeds this quarter's pipeline; demand gen feeds future quarters
  • Over-indexing on lead gen exhausts "ready to buy" prospects without building future pipeline
  • Track both separately‚Äîand advocate for demand gen metrics beyond leads
üìç Scenario: The Podcast Problem
SP

Sarah Park

Demand Gen Manager, NovaTech

"I want to launch a podcast. Derek asked me how many leads it would generate. When I said 'probably zero direct leads,' he looked at me like I was crazy."

Sarah had been pushing for a podcast for months. She knew the audience was there‚Äîengineering leaders who spent their commutes listening to industry content. But Derek wanted leads.

"How do I justify the budget if we can't show ROI?" Derek asked.

Maya pulled up a dashboard. "Look at our self-reported attribution from the last 6 months. 34% of demo requests selected 'Podcast' when asked how they heard about us. But we don't have a podcast."

The room went silent.

"They're listening to other podcasts that mention us," Maya explained. "Competitors have shows. Industry analysts host us as guests. People hear about us, then Google us later. The attribution shows up as 'Direct' or 'Organic Search' in GA4, but they self-report podcasts as the actual source."

Sarah jumped in: "And when we gate everything, we're only capturing people ready to buy RIGHT NOW. The podcast builds our audience for next quarter, and the quarter after that."

üí° THE TAKEAWAY

Lead gen feeds today's pipeline. Demand gen builds tomorrow's. If you only do lead gen, you'll eventually exhaust everyone ready to buy and wonder why the well ran dry. Both matter‚Äîbut they measure differently.

The Two Approaches Compared

  • Webinar registrations

  • Content syndication
  • Trade show lead capture

üìä Key Differences
Aspect Lead Gen Demand Gen

Finding the Right Mix

Most B2B companies need both. The ratio depends on your business model:

  • Enterprise (12-month cycles, $100K+ ACV): 70% demand gen / 30% lead gen
  • Mid-market (3-6 month cycles): 50/50 balance
  • SMB/PLG (30-day cycles): 30% demand gen / 70% lead gen
Deep Dive: Measuring Demand Gen

Since demand gen doesn't produce forms fills, you need proxy metrics:

Leading indicators:

  • Branded search volume: More people searching for your company name = awareness growing
  • Direct traffic: People typing your URL directly
  • Self-reported attribution: "How did you hear about us?" on forms
  • Share of voice: Mentions vs. competitors

How to advocate for demand gen budget:

  1. Add "How did you hear about us?" to demo request forms
  2. Track branded search trends over time
  3. Show correlation between demand gen activity and future pipeline
  4. Highlight competitors investing in brand (they know something)
‚úÖ Knowledge Check

Your company gates all content and does 100% lead gen. Pipeline is strong this quarter, but 6sense shows your share of voice is declining. What's the likely future risk?

100% lead gen means you're only capturing people in-market RIGHT NOW. With declining share of voice, fewer people will think of you when they enter the market. Today's pipeline comes from awareness built 6-12 months ago. If you're not building awareness now, you're borrowing from future quarters. This is a common trap‚Äîpipeline looks great until it suddenly doesn't.

"Content syndication is generating leads at $35 each. LinkedIn is $120 per lead. Why are we spending anything on LinkedIn?"

üí° THE TAKEAWAY

CPL is a vanity metric. What matters is cost per pipeline dollar or cost per customer. A channel that looks cheap at lead gen might be expensive by the time you account for conversion rates through the full funnel.

Key Metrics #

The numbers you'll calculate, report on, and be asked about constantly

TL;DR
  • Funnel metrics track volume (leads, MQLs, SQLs) and conversion rates between stages
  • Efficiency metrics (CAC, LTV, LTV:CAC ratio) show if you're acquiring customers profitably
  • Marketing-specific metrics (CPL, pipeline sourced/influenced) prove marketing's revenue impact
  • 3:1 to 5:1 LTV:CAC is the healthy range - lower means spending too much, higher means underinvesting
NovaTech Scenario

Maya Chen (MOps) has her quarterly board prep meeting in two days. The CMO asks: "I need a slide showing our unit economics - CAC, LTV, payback period. And compare it to last quarter."

Conversion Rates

The percentage of records that progress from one stage to the next.

Conversion Rate = (Records entering next stage) / (Records in current stage) √ó 100

Example:
MQL to SQL Rate = SQLs / MQLs √ó 100
If you had 200 MQLs and 80 became SQLs: 80/200 = 40% conversion rate

Pipeline Velocity

How fast deals move through your pipeline. Measured in days per stage or overall cycle time.

Sales Cycle Length = Close Date - Opportunity Create Date

Average Sales Cycle = Sum of all cycle lengths / Number of closed deals

Stage Velocity = Average days spent in each stage

Efficiency Metrics

Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC)

Total cost to acquire a new customer.

CAC = (Sales + Marketing Costs) / New Customers Acquired

Example:
Q1 marketing spend: $500,000
Q1 sales spend: $800,000
Q1 new customers: 50

CAC = ($500,000 + $800,000) / 50 = $26,000 per customer
Common mistake: Calculating CAC with only marketing costs. True CAC includes sales salaries, commissions, tools, and overhead. Marketing CAC (just marketing costs) is a useful subset but shouldn't be confused with full CAC.

Lifetime Value (LTV)

Total revenue expected from a customer over their lifetime.

Simple LTV = (Average Annual Contract Value) √ó (Average Customer Lifespan in Years)

Or with churn:
LTV = ARPA / Churn Rate

Example:
Average ACV: $50,000
Average lifespan: 4 years (or 25% annual churn ‚Üí 1/0.25 = 4 years)

LTV = $50,000 √ó 4 = $200,000

LTV:CAC Ratio

How efficiently you're acquiring customers.

LTV:CAC = LTV / CAC

Example:
LTV = $200,000
CAC = $26,000

LTV:CAC = $200,000 / $26,000 = 7.7:1
LTV:CAC Ratio Interpretation
Ratio Interpretation Action
<3:1 Spending too much to acquire Reduce CAC or increase retention
3:1 - 5:1 Healthy range Maintain current approach
>5:1 Under-investing in growth Could spend more to acquire

CAC Payback Period

How long until you recover the cost of acquiring a customer.

CAC Payback = CAC / (ACV √ó Gross Margin)

Example:
CAC = $26,000
ACV = $50,000
Gross Margin = 80%

CAC Payback = $26,000 / ($50,000 √ó 0.80) = 0.65 years = ~8 months

Benchmark: 12-18 months is typical for B2B SaaS. Under 12 months is excellent. Over 24 months is concerning.

Marketing-Specific Metrics

Cost Per Lead (CPL) & Cost Per MQL

CPL = Marketing Spend on Channel / Leads Generated

Cost per MQL = Marketing Spend / MQLs Generated
            (or) CPL / Lead-to-MQL Conversion Rate

Example:
LinkedIn spend: $50,000
Leads from LinkedIn: 500
Lead-to-MQL rate: 20%

CPL = $50,000 / 500 = $100 per lead
Cost per MQL = $100 / 0.20 = $500 per MQL

Benchmarks: B2B SaaS CPL: $50-200 typical for paid channels. Enterprise: $200-500+.

Marketing-Sourced vs. Marketing-Influenced Pipeline

Marketing-Sourced Pipeline = Sum of opportunity values where
                             opportunity source = marketing channel

Marketing-Influenced Pipeline = Sum of opportunity values where
                                any contact at the account engaged
                                with marketing before opportunity close
Why this matters for analysts: Marketing-influenced is always higher than marketing-sourced. Some companies report both; others only report one. Know which metric is being discussed and be consistent. Marketing-sourced is more conservative; marketing-influenced captures more of marketing's actual impact but can be gamed.

Deep Dive: The Metrics That Actually Get You Promoted

Every metric above matters, but some have outsized impact on your career. Here's the hierarchy:

Tier 1: The Board-Level Metrics

These are what executives care about:

  • Pipeline coverage ratio: Open pipeline √∑ Revenue target. 3x-4x coverage is healthy.
  • LTV:CAC: Proves the business model works.
  • Marketing-sourced revenue: Hard proof of marketing's value.

Tier 2: The Optimization Levers

These help you find problems and opportunities:

  • Stage conversion rates: Where is the funnel leaking?
  • CPL by channel: Where should we invest more/less?

Tier 3: Diagnostic Metrics

Useful for troubleshooting but not for reporting:

  • Email open/click rates
  • Page views per session

The "No Vanity Metrics" Rule

For every metric you report, ask: "If this number changed, what would we do differently?" If the answer is "nothing" or "I don't know," it's a vanity metric. Kill it.

Examples of vanity metrics masquerading as real metrics:

  • Total website traffic (unless you can tie it to pipeline)
  • Social media followers (unless they convert)
  • Email list size (unengaged subscribers are worthless)
  • Number of MQLs (if SQL rate is terrible)

An 8:1 LTV:CAC ratio is above the "healthy" range of 3:1-5:1. This suggests the company could afford to spend more on acquisition while still maintaining profitable unit economics‚Äîthey're being too conservative with growth investment.
